Motivation to change!

Unfortunately, the harsh reality is most Portfolio Managers don’t have the drive to change their age-old habits. No motivation to research their own behavioural biases, create a process to practice or to overhaul their undifferentiated investment philosophy. These skill enhancers aren’t done because not doing them is easier than acting. Volatility and uncertainty are an unavoidable part of a job as a Portfolio Manager

By way of example and surprisingly (at least to me), the average annual volatility of Amazon stock over its 25-year price history is around 33% and the average annual price increase is around 33%!

Before any transition from an analyst to an investor, IMHO, it is now absolutely fundamental to help new PM’s assess the level of risk they are willing to tolerate. Most investment firms don’t consider whether a potential PM can likely cope with the volatility of an Amazon but having helped many analysts navigate the transition to portfolio manager, awareness of individual risk temperament is becoming a ‘must have’ for professional investment platforms.

All adult humans have a natural risk appetite. This doesn’t change throughout life. It derives from how ‘emotional’ we tend to be (on a scale of anxious to intrepid) overlayed with how we tend to cognitively process (on a scale of organised to spontaneous). Thus, to understand your default risk appetite, you can’t separate reason from emotion. It is like a location on a risk map. Using a specific psychometric tool it is now possible to pinpoint where each of us sit in terms of 8 risk types on this map. No one risk type is optimal for a PM but awareness of your own type helps us cope under uncertainty and in dealing with bad outcomes. Diversity of risk types in teams are important insights as no single person can embody the risk dispositions of all 8 risk types. It is a fascinating tool for PMs and in the face of the current extreme volatility a real advantage for investment teams. Analysts wishing to be PMs should ask their firms for support.

Don't be the Swiss army knife of investors

Too many investors confuse familiarity with competence The circle of competence filter is so simple it is embarrassing to say it out loud, but here goes: ‘When you do not know what you are doing, it is riskier than when you do know what you are doing.’ What an equity investor needs is the ability to correctly evaluate selected businesses. Note that word “selected”: You don’t have to be an expert on every sub-sector or company.

You only have to be able to evaluate companies within your circle of competence. The size of that circle is not very important; knowing its boundaries, however, is vital. Stock ideas aren’t the only thing that can be in your circle of competence, people can be too. One of the best ways to make an investment team work much better is match people who complement each other in strengths and weaknesses. It’s really powerful to ask yourself what you think you know versus what you actually know both for your own investment process and perhaps even more importantly about the people in your investment team.
